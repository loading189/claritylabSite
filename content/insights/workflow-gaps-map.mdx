Most service operators do not lose jobs because one person failed. They lose margin and speed in the spaces between steps.

Estimate looks good. Schedule looks full. But jobs stall, closeout data is incomplete, and invoicing drifts. The leak lives in handoffs.

## Build a simple handoff map

Take one whiteboard and map four stages:

- Estimate created
- Job scheduled
- Job completed
- Invoice sent

For each stage, answer three questions:

- What must be true before this can move forward?
- Who confirms it?
- Where is that confirmation recorded?

If a stage has unclear ownership or undocumented criteria, that is a leak point.

## The 30-day gap-closing sprint

Week 1: identify top three recurring failure modes from last month.

Week 2: define minimum handoff standards and publish them to team leads.

Week 3: run daily 10-minute standup focused only on blocked handoffs.

Week 4: review lag metrics and remove one unnecessary approval step.

Most teams improve just by making handoff rules explicit.

## Metrics that prove your map is working

Choose a few outcome measures tied to handoffs:

- % jobs scheduled with complete scope notes
- % jobs closed same day with full documentation
- Days from job completion to invoice
- % invoices requiring rework

These are operational leading indicators for cash. If they improve, AR pressure usually eases next.

## Common trap: adding tools instead of rules

When teams feel workflow pain, they often buy another platform. New tools can help, but only after handoff standards are defined.

Without standards, software just digitizes confusion.

Before adding tooling, test this question: "Could a new supervisor run this process tomorrow using our current documentation?" If the answer is no, start with process clarity.
